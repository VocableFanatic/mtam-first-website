The overview of linguistics rights in Brazil

Linguistic rights

In Brazil, language rights are intrinsically linked to the diversity of languages spoken in the country, with a special focus on indigenous languages and the Brazilian Sign Language (Libras). They comprise several fundamental dimensions, among which we can highlight:

Indigenous languages are officially recognized and protected by the Brazilian Constitution. Indigenous peoples have the right to use their languages in educational, administrative and cultural contexts, as established in Law No. 9.394/1996 and Law No. 11.645/2008, which makes the teaching of indigenous history and culture compulsory in schools.

The Brazilian Sign Language (Libras), used by the deaf community in Brazil, is legally recognized as a means of communication and expression through Law 10.436/2002, regulated by Decree 5.626/2005.

In the context of indigenous peoples, there are bilingual schools that teach in both indigenous languages and Portuguese, as well as in sign language (Libras) and Portuguese. This plays a crucial role in the preservation of the languages in question.

For information, languages of migratory origin such as Talian, Pomeranian and Hunrisquean are currently considered by IPHAN to be Brazilian intangible cultural heritage. This measure is important for strengthening the preservation, research and teaching of these languages. However, the measures adopted for education lack a single support system in the country.
