Uchinaa

Location of Ryukyuu islands

Uchinaa was a sovereign kingdom with its own traditions and languages. Many of them were strongly influenced by contact with other East Asian kingdoms.

Located on theRyukyu u/Nansei islands in the south-west of the Japanese archipelago today the site is the prefecture of Okinawa, a territory annexed in 1872 by the Japanese empire.

Various measures to erase and repress local culture were ordered, including the imposition of the Japanese language in the territory and a ban on the speaking of native languages on the island, affecting everything from the teaching of the language in schools to religious manifestations.

With the advent of World War II, it came under US control and was called Ryukyuu. During this period, violence further reinforced the contempt for the island's culture and inhabitants. In 1972, it was reintegrated into Japan.

It is important to note (TOMA, 2022) that the name Okinawa, given by the Japanese, is "one of the different names that the archipelago has received, each with a different historical and political meaning."

Uchinaa, on the other hand, is the name given to the indigenous peoples (Uchinachus) who have been recognized as such by the UN since 1989.

Many of the consequences of this double colonialism persist, since Uchinaaguchi (the language of Uchinaa) or Okinawan is classified by UNESCO as being in serious danger of extinction.

Migration to Brazil

Kasato Maru at the port of Santos in 1908.

42% of the immigrants who came from Japan in 1908 on the Kasato Maru, the first Japanese ship to begin the period of Japanese immigration to Brazil, were from Okinawa.

Today, immigrants and their descendants, classified as Nikkei, make up 10 per cent of the Japanese community. This corresponds to 150,000 Okinawans out of the one and a half million Japanese in Brazil.

As this prohibition policy did not exist in Brazil, immigrants continued to communicate in their mother tongue and passed it on to their descendants

Due to the policies adopted, it is possible that the language currently has more speakers in Brazil than in Japan.

Several associations promote Okinawan culture and language in Brazil. In São Paulo, for example, Colégio Exatus offers the Okinawan course free of charge to those interested.

In the Federal District, the agricultural colony of Vargem Bonita has an association (kaikan) that promotes cultural and gastronomic events, sports activities and is the headquarters of the taiko (drums) group Ryukyu Koku Matsuri Daiko （琉球國祭り太鼓) - Brasília branch.
